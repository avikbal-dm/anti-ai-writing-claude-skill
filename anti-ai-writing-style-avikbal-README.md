# Anti-AI Writing Style, the Humanizer

A writing-style ruleset by Avik Bal. It makes model-assisted writing read like a real person wrote it, and it keeps that writing honest. Use it to draft in this voice, or as an edit pass that strips the tells out of an existing draft.

## Why this exists

AI made content cheap and identical. Every brand now runs the same models on the same prompts, and the result is a web filling up with prose that is grammatically perfect and completely forgettable. Readers skim past it. Search and AI engines discount it. The thing that is suddenly scarce is content that sounds like a specific person who knows something.

So humanising is not a cosmetic step you do at the end. It is the work. The challenge is not getting a model to write. Any model writes. The challenge is getting writing that carries a real point of view, real detail, and a voice a reader trusts. That is what this skill solves.

It does it two ways. It removes the patterns that mark text as machine-made, the inflated vocabulary, the reframe sentences, the fake depth, the metronome rhythm. And it forces the opposite of generic: real inputs, specific numbers, genuine opinion. The second half matters more than the first. A draft with the tells removed is clean. A draft with your real material in it is yours.

## What it does

- Runs in two modes: draft a piece from scratch, or humanize an existing draft.
- Kills the AI tells: banned vocabulary, bloated verbs, dead openings and transitions, negative parallelism and reframe sentences, forced analogies, significance inflation, and same-length rhythm.
- Forces specificity: real numbers, names, examples, and tradeoffs over vague claims.
- Asks you for the inputs only you have, and never fabricates facts, data, or examples.
- Matches your own voice when you give it samples.
- Ends with a silent cleanup pass.

## The workflow

Use it in this order. The order is the point.

1. Get the substance right first. Decide what you are actually saying and who it is for. Style cannot save an empty argument.
2. Hand over your real material, or let the skill ask for it. Your numbers, a real example from your work, your honest opinion, the thing most people in your field get wrong.
3. Draft, or paste the draft you want humanized.
4. Let the skill run its cleanup pass and remove the tells.
5. Read it aloud. Anything you would not say out loud gets cut or rewritten.
6. Add the one detail only you could have written. This is the step people skip, and it is the one that makes the piece yours.
7. Publish.

A draft that went through steps 1 to 7 reads like you. A draft that skipped 2 and 6 reads like a clean model. The difference is visible in the first paragraph.

## How to make it more human and more personal

The skill gets you to clean and specific. These habits get you to unmistakably yours.

- Feed it your own writing. Paste two or three samples and tell it to match your rhythm and vocabulary. It will prioritize your real voice over its defaults.
- Keep a swipe file. Save your recurring phrases, your strong opinions, and three or four real stories from your work. Drop the relevant ones into the prompt.
- Always answer its questions with specifics. When it asks for a number or an example, give a real one. Vague answers produce vague writing.
- Lead with a genuine opinion. State where you disagree with the common take and why. A model will not do this on its own, because it averages. You are not an average.
- Write in first person and name real things. Real clients where you are allowed, real tools, real dates, real outcomes. Specificity is the cheapest authenticity there is.
- Do the last ten percent by hand. The final human edit, a cut line here, a sharper open there, is what no model reproduces.
- Do not write to beat AI detectors. They are unreliable and they flag clean human writing constantly. Chasing a score makes your sentences worse. Write to be specific and true, and the question answers itself.

## How this connects to CITED

CITED is Avik Bal's framework for how a business gets found, trusted, and chosen when machines answer the questions. It runs in five layers, Citation Surface, Information Architecture, Technical Foundation, Evidence-Rich Extraction, and Demand Capture, turned by the Citation Flywheel.

This skill is the part that makes the content worth citing.

CITED fixes where and how you show up. It gets your pages structured, technically sound, and positioned to be pulled into AI answers. But structure around hollow content earns nothing. AI engines cite material that is specific, evidence-backed, and clearly written by someone who knows the subject. Generic model prose, the kind every competitor is also publishing, gives an engine no reason to choose you.

The tightest link is to the fourth layer, Evidence-Rich Extraction. That layer depends on content an engine can lift and quote: a real number, a clear definition, a concrete example, a defensible claim. Those are exactly the things this skill forces into the writing and refuses to fake. Remove the AI tells, add the real inputs, and you produce the extractable, trustworthy passages that layer is built to capture.

It also feeds the Flywheel. Authentic, specific content earns citations. Citations build authority. Authority earns more citations. Generic content never starts the wheel turning, because nothing about it is worth pointing to.

The practical sequence is simple. Run CITED so that when you show up, you show up in the right place, in the right structure. Run this skill so that when you show up, the writing deserves the citation.
